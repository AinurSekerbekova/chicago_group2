About the Project

This section explores the background, design, and heritage goals of the Chicago Fire digital storytelling project.

Dimension 1 — Social Impact: The Great Chicago Fire (October 1871) killed 300 people, left 90,000 homeless, and destroyed $200 million in property. This remains one of history’s most devastating urban disasters—relevant today as cities face climate vulnerability and disaster recovery challenges.

Dimension 2 — Available Evidence: Seven first-hand letters from survivors written during or immediately after the fire provide authentic voices from different social positions:

  • Jonas Hutchinson (lawyer) → institutional collapse
  • Mary Fales (lawyer’s wife) → family displacement
  • George Howland (school principal) → loss of knowledge
  • Aurelia R. King (merchant’s family) → survival and faith
  • Anna E. Higginson (real estate broker’s wife) → preservation consciousness
  • Adeline Rossiter Judd (volunteer helper) → mutual aid
  • Samuel Stone (assistant librarian) → vision for rebuilding

Dimension 3 — Geographic Specificity: Mapping seven physical locations in modern Chicago where each letter-writer stood during the fire creates embodied learning — players discover that glass skyscrapers now stand where people fled through flames.

Dimension 4 — Narrative Power: The story moves from destruction → survival → rebuilding. This isn't just tragedy; it's a resilience narrative where Chicago refused to disappear.

Dimension 5 — Audience Engagement: Four narrator-guides + interactive gameplay mechanics + gamification attract younger audiences who ignore traditional museum exhibits.

Step 1 — Conceptualization (Design): Research question: “How can interactive digital storytelling using survivor narratives reshape public understanding and emotional engagement with historical urban disasters such as the Great Chicago Fire?”

Step 2 — Digital Tool Selection: Narrative design, geospatial mapping, and content development using QGIS, Visual Studio Code, Adobe Premiere and LLMs for immersive historical sequences.

Step 3 — Implementation: GitHub repository structure includes game engine, narrative content, and geospatial data.

Step 4 — Heritage Integration: Links to the Chicago History Museum’s digital archive and educational deployment in curricula.

  1. Preservation Against Decay: 150-year-old paper documents are fragile. Digital copies in cloud storage protect against fire, flood, and degradation.
  2. Global Accessibility: Physical letters locked in archives become available worldwide via smartphone, making Chicago's history meaningful across borders.
  3. Enhanced Interpretation: Static text becomes emotionally vivid through immersive storytelling — voice acting, lighting, and spatial context turn reading into experiencing.
  4. Intangible Heritage: The letters were originally oral stories told around fires. The game returns them to that format — voice, emotion, community.
  5. Educational Reuse: Digital formats enable reuse for documentaries, academic research, museum exhibits, and tourism apps.
